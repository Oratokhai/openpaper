1,240 words

The day the
benchmark lied

I had a harness I trusted. Two hundred graded tasks, a fixed rubric, a number I could quote in a standup. Then I swapped the model and the number went up by nine points overnight — and I almost shipped that result without reading a single transcript.

The first rule of evals nobody tells you: a score that moves the way you wanted is the most dangerous score there is. So before I believed it, I went back to the prompt that produced the jump.

Prompt · grader

You are grading a model's answer against a reference. Return PASS only if every claim in the answer is supported by the reference. Be strict. Think step by step, then output a single token.

The model hadn't gotten smarter. It had gotten better at sounding like the rubric — and my grader, reading for confidence, rewarded the performance. The benchmark didn't lie. I did, by trusting a delta I never inspected.
